Chapter 25: The Macroeconomic Aims of the Government

1. Economic growth

  • an increase in the output of an economy over time

  • In the long run, the productive potential / capacity need to be increased to sustain the growth.

    1. improve the quality or quantity of FOC
    2. PPC shift to right
    3. productivity capacity rises
    4. 'potential' growth
    • immigration / deregulation (more firms) / labour market participations / innovation / investment / education

Aggregate demand

  • the total demand for a country's products at a given price level.
  • C+I+G+(X−M)
  • C: consumption I: investment G: gov spending X-M: net exports

Aggregate supply (Keynesian Model)

  • the total amount of goods and services that domestic firms are willing and able to sell at a given level.

  • perfectly elastic if the economy has many unemplyed resources.

  • more inelastic as the economy approaches "full employment" until perfectly inelastic.

    How to increase AS

    • Higher productivity of labour and capital
    • Growing population and increased labout market participation
    • Innovation and enterprise
    • capital investment
    • Stock of natural resources

     

Graph of "actual economic growth" (Keynesian Model)

  • only one AS curve unlike SRAS/LRAS in Classical model
  • Economy operating at where AD meets AS, before reaching full employment
  • R = "real GDP"
  • when AD rises, there is a rise in the country's real GDP level.

Graph of potential economic growth (Keynesian Model)

  • nothing happened to real GDP
  • Economy operating before reaching full employment
  • caused by a rise in quality or quantity in resources

Graph of "full capacity" (Keynesian Model)

  • when AD rises, price level rises a lot (P1-P2) and small change in real GDP. (Y1-YFE) FE = full employment

  • Difficult to increase actual outpul by simply increasing AD.

    AS need to be increased.

PPC vs AS Curve

  • both show a potencial and real output
  • a shift in both curve show that potential output of a nation increases
  • changes in real GDP/real growth of an economy are shown in the AD/AS model as shifts of the AD/AS curve
  • the PPC is a movement of an indicator showing the situation of the econom

Classical Model

  • In the long run, LRAS is vertical at full employment .
  • A change in AD only cause price change.
  • The real GDP is only determined by a shift in LRAS

Reasons why gov aim for economic growth

  • Raise living standards
  • Employment is likely to rise
  • If output increases to match demand, upward pressure on the price level can be avoided.
  • Improve trade position by extra exports.
  • Some of the poor may have job and others may be helped by extra tax revenue.

2. Low Unemployment

  • People who are willing and able to work at the suitable wage rate that do no have a job are "unemployed"

  • Labour force: economically active people who are able and willing to work

    Does not include e.g. children, full time students, retired people, prisoners

  • Unemployment rate = UnemploymentLabourforce×100

  • 0% unemployment is not possible as there will always be some workers changing jobs and being unemployed for short periods.

    natural rate of unemployment

Reasons to aim for low unemployment

  • Unemployment is a waste of resources, as the economy is still having a “spare capacity”
  1. Low income unable to satisfy basic needs and wants
  2. High gov spending on unemployment benifits. Opportunity Costs.
  3. Lower tax revenue
  4. Low consumption in the economy leading to possible further recession.

3. Price Stability

  • the price level in the economy not changing significantly over time.

  • Not aiming for 0% price change

  • Rise in inflation tend to overstate rises in prices.

    • Price people paid might be lower than official indices because of reduced prices in sales and second-hand purchases.
    • Hide improvements in product

  • Slight rise in general price level has benifits

    • Encourage producers to increase output
    • Enable firms to cut wage costs by not raising wages with inflation

  • Gov avoid a fall in price level if caused by fall in AD

    • Decline in output and rise in unemployment

Reasons to stable price level

  • Ensures greater economic certainly and prevents the country's products from losing international competitiveness
  • Consumers can plan with greater confidence
  • Firms not raise prices
  • Households not bring forward purchases for fear.
  • Workers not press for wage increase

4. Balance of payment stability

  • The value of exports equals to the value of imports

  • Import > export

    • Debt
    • Not worried if caused by importing raw materials and capital goods.

  • Export > import

    • Citizens not enjoying as many products

5. Income redistribution

  • A gov may seek to redistribute income form the rich to the poor.

    • By taxing and spending

  • Some tax revenue raised is directly spend on the poor.

    • Housing/unemployment benifit

Reasons to redistribute income

  • Rich will be richer without intervention
  • Social problems might be caused if a significant gap between the rich and the poor
  • Unlikely to aim for perfectly equal distribution.

 

Conflicts

Why Low Unemployment rate -> High inflation

  • Increase consumer spending - higher demand - higher consumption - firm employ more
  • Low unemployment - less unemployed people - firm need to compete on labour market - higher wages to attract workers - higher cost of production - higher prices

Economic growth vs balance of payment stability

  • Lower income tax rate - higher disposable income - spend more - BUT spend more on imports/oversea holidays - trade deficit (M rises and X-M falls)
  • Econ growth of exports abroad result in a trade surplus

 

Chapter 26: Fiscal Policy

Budget

  • The relationship between government revenue and government spending.
  • Budget deficit: spending higher than revenue (usually in economic recession)
  • Budget surplus: revenue higher than spending (usually in economic boom)
  • Budget balance: GS=GR
  • National debt: the total amount of a government has borrowed over time.

Reasons for government spending

  • To provide essential public services.
  • To provide merit goods. To make them affordable and accessible. Reduce inequality and increase the living standard of the population.
  • To provide public goods. Would not be provided at out without gov due to free rider problem.
  • To promote income and wealth equality. e.g. unemployment benefit
  • To pay interest on national debt.
  • To influence economic activity. e.g. increase spending in order to increase aggregate demand.

Problems with Fiscal Policy

  • Too much public spending can cause inflation
  • Increase in taxes can reduce work incentives
  • Public spending can crowd out private spending.

The multiplier effect

  • An initial increase on spending will cause a greater rise in AD
  • $20 -> $15 -> $ 10 -> $5 = $45
  • Formula: change in spending×the multiplier=change in national income

Reasons of levying tax

  • To raise government revenue
  • To discourage the consumption of demerit goods
  • To discourage the provision os demerit goods.
  • Protect domestic industries: placing tariffs
  • To redistribute income form the rich to the poor: progressive taxes
  • To influence the economic activity: to change AD

Direct and Indirect tax

Direct Tax

  • Tax on income and wealth.

  • Calculates as a proportion of income

  • Types

    • Income tax(earnings)
    • Corporation tax(profits)
    • Capital gains tax(shares transactions)
    • Inheritance Tax (Wealth when passed on to other people)

Indirect Tax

  • Tax on expenditure.

  • When individuals spend, a portion of the price they pay goes to the gov.

  • Unit tax

  • Types

    • Sales Tax e.g. VAT (Value adds tax)
    • Excise Duties charged on certain domestically produced goods e.g. alcohol, petrol, cigarette. Charged in addition to VAT
    • Customs duties "import tariffs"

Demand and Supply diagrams only demonstrates indirect tax.

Who would pay more?

  • Demand Elastic

    • Suppliers pay more

  • Demand Inelastic

    • Consumers pay more

Progressive Tax, Regressive Tax and Proportional Tax

  • Progressive Tax

    • Higher proportion of income from the rich than the poor.
    • Based on 'ability to pay'

  • Regressive tax

    • Higher proportion of income from the poor than the rich.
    • More impact on lower income people.
    • e.g. fixed tax rate

  • Proportional Tax

    • A tax system that takes an equal proportion of income from all income groups.

Local taxes

  • Used to pay for local services such as education, fire services, roads.

  • Based On

    • The property of local firms
    • The value of household property e.g. council tax

 

Principles of tax(important)

  • Equity: fairness. The amount of of tax paid should be based on 'ability to pay'
  • Certainty: easy to understand, households and firms able to calculate themselves. Taxpayer should know exactly how and when.
  • Convenience: easy to pay. Multiple payment methods available, such as cheque/internet banking.
  • Economy: cost of collecting tax considerably lower than the revenue it generates
  • Flexibility: possible to change tax if economic activity or gov aim change. Adjusts to offset economic booms and slumps
  • Efficiency: to improve the performance of markets, at least not significantly reduce the efficiency of markets. e.g.' Windfall' charged on supernormal profits. Tax on pollution. Income Tax rate should not too high.

 

Tax evasion and Tax avoidance

  • Tax evasion :

    • Illegal non-payment of taxes to the government

  • Tax avoidance:

    • legal process of finding different ways to minimize the amount of tax paid to the government

Fiscal Policy

  • Decision on gov spending and taxation designed to influence aggregate demand in an economy.
  • AD=C+I+G+(X−M)

Expansionary Fiscal Policy

  • Increase in gov spending and a reduction in income tax rates to increase AD.
  • Reach Econ growth and lower down unemployment rate.

Contradictory Fiscal Policy

  • Drop in gov spending and a rise in income tax rates to decrease AD.
  • Keep inflation rate low and stable. Ensure 'balance of payment' stability.

 

Fiscal Policy affects Income Equality

  • Increased employment opportunities: More spending and less income tax - higher AD - firms hire more workers - earn higher incomes
  • Spending on education and healthcare: higher productivity - higher paid jobs
  • Progressive tax highers
  • Regressive tax lower: e.g. remove direct taxes on basic necessities

Example: Building a new public school

  • Lower unemployment: create new job vacancies for teachers and cleaners...
  • Higher economic growth: Materials and equipment might be purchased from local businesses to build this school, contributing to increased production and higher economic growth. Also, new teachers having higher income level could spend more, C rises!
  • Higher inflation: AD goes up leading to “demand-pull” inflation.
  • Trade deficit on the balance of payment: people with higher income might spend more on buying imports.

 

 

Chapter 27: Monetary Policy

  • Monetary policy: decisions on the money supply, the interest rate and the exchange rate taken to influence aggregate demand.

    • Usually carried out by the central bank on behalf of gov

  • Money Supply: The total quantity of money in circulation in the economy

    • Narrow measure: coins, notes, and current accounts.
    • Broad measure: Above+Deposit account.

1. Change in money supply

  • Printing money:"resorting to the printing press" increase money supply directly.
  • Buying gov bonds: give commercial banks more money to lend.
  • Encouraging commercial banks to lend money.: removing restrictions. Encouraging consumers and firms to borrow more and more consumption and investment.

2. Change in interest rate

  • Central bank alters the base rate

    • Commercial banks increase interest rate charged. Cost of borrowing increase.

  • Central bank may keep interest rate unchanged to promote certainty and and make planning easier.

    • Certainty encourage more investment, both domestic and foreign.

  • Rise in interest rate would increase cost of borrowing and reduce AD via:

    1. Any households who have borrowed before need to pay back more interest. This reduce their willingness and ability to invest and consume.
    2. New borrowings more expensive.
    3. Increase incentives to save. Households and firms earn more from saving. OC of investing would be increased.

3. Change in currency exchange rate (seldom used)

  • Devaluation: a fall in the value of the domestic currency as a result of gov intervention.

    • Make exports cheaper and imports more expensive
    • Higher export revenue (X)
    • Lower spending (M) as consumers choose cheaper domestic goods over expensive imports.
    • Higher net export -> Higher AD
    • Encourage more foreign MNC to set up operations in the country.
    • Higher inflation.

Expansionary Monetary Policy

  • the use of increase in money supply and decrease of interest rate to boost AD in the economy.
  • Lower interest rate, lower the cost of borrowing.
  • Households and firms borrow more.
  • Higher AD-higher Econ growth- lower unemployment.
  • Contractionary Monetary Policy is the opposite.

 

Positive relationship between interest rate and exchange rate

  • A change in interest rates will affect the return that foreign investors receive on the investment they are holding in a country.
  • Higher interest rate - foreign investors benefit from their investments - invest more - "hot money"appear - demand for currency goes up - D shift to the right - increase the price of currency - appreciation.

Redistributing income

  • Positive: Expansionary monetary policy make more equal

    • Increase money supply-increase AD-more production-more job vacancies which benefit poor people-unemployment rate fall-poor people earn more

  • Negative rising income not spread evenly across population, leading to widening income gap

    • Rise in inflation rate-decline in the real value of income-poor people get little access to basic products as they are more expensive
    • Rich people's assets are less affected.

Chapter 28 Supply-side policy

  • Fiscal Policy + Monetary Policy = Demand-side policy
  • A measure taken by the gov to increase AS (total quantity produced) and productive capacity.

1. Education and training

  • Improve worker's knowledge and skills over time
  • Increase labour productivity, thus improve the quality of product produced.
  • Firms sell more so they could expand - productive capacity rises
  • Supply become more elastic
  • Knowledge might not be related

2. Labour market reform

  • Increase the quality, quantity and flexibility of labour.

    • Better training - higher quality of training - higher productive capacity

  • Removal of barriers to entry (qualifications) and exit(long contracts): make it easier for people to join in an industry and work - higher quantity

  • Make it easier to hire and fire workers. However, firms might not spend a lot of time on training as they think workers only required for short time.

  • Reform of trade unions: if the power is reduced, they may be less inclined to engage in industrial action.

    • Less disruption to production
    • Rise in quality of resource so rise in productive capacity
    Drawbacks of a strong trade union
    • Cannot fire redundant workers
    • Wage rise, cost of production rise
    • Strikes reduce efficiency, income and reputation

3. Lower direct taxes and increase incentives.

  • "Lower direct taxation" is Expansionary fiscal policy
  • Lower income tax increase disposable income and may act as an incentive.
  • Lower corporate tax higher profits received by entrepreneurs might encourage them to invest in starting up new businesses.
  • Increase the productive capacity of the economy by increasing AS.

4. Increasing competition

Deregulation

  • the removal of laws, rules and regulations, making it easier for a business to enter or operate in an industry.
  • Encourage competition, forcing firms to lower costs and increase productivity
  • Reduce the cost for gov
  • If deregulation does increase competition, it may increase efficiency and lower costs of production.
  • Higher chance of monopoly

Privatization

  • when ownership of a public sector business is transferred to the private sector.
  • Private owners more likely to run business more efficiently due to profit motive and high competition.

5. Subsidies and Grants

  • Encourage entrepreneurs to start up new businesses.

  • Subsidies can also be provided to existing businesses to encourage them to innovate. There are mainly two types of innovation

    Product innovation

    • a subsidy on R&D may encourage firms to produce new and improved goods and services which better meet the needs and wants of consumers.
    • Compete more effectively with foreign firms, and increase the prod. capacity of the economy.

    Process innovation

    • Develop new technology, which lower costs by improving the efficiency of the production process.
    • This improvement in the quality of capital increases productivity, thereby increasing AD in the economy.

6. Improving infrastructure

  • Facilitate effective production and minimize waste.

 

Effects of supply-side policy

  • In the long run, all gov's marcoecon aims have the potential to benefit from supply-side policy.

  • Increase AS can cause the economy to grow without inflation.

  • Some supply-side policy could be expensive, like building schools

  • Some supply-side policy might involve a TIME LAG. Tak time before the effect of improved edu and privatization are experienced.

    Why time lag is bad?
    • Gov spend money to build schools - higher AD - if already at full employment - higher inflationary pressure.

 

Effects on gov's macroeconomic aims

By improving the quantity and quality of resources, supply-side policies make it possible for the government to increase the amount of goods and services that an economy is able to supply.

  • Increase AS and productive potential, leading to lower inflation, higher Econ growth and lower unemployment

  • Improving edu - more skilled and productive workforce overtime - cost to supply goods decrease, leading to lower cost-push inflation.

  • THEREFORE sustained, non-inflationary Econ growth.

    Redistributing Income
    • Create jobs, raise income of low-skilled workers, reduce income gap.
    • Effectiveness depends on the types of jobs created, if job unskilled, not reduce the gap.
    • In developing countries might lead to widening income gap.
    • Improved education and training
    • Lower income taxes to raise incentive.
    • Subsidies on essential goods

 

Chapter 29 Economic growth

  • the increase in the value of goods and services produced in an economy over time
  • Often measured GDP.

GDP Calculations

  • GDP: total output produced in a country.

  1. Expenditure Method

    • Adds up all the expenditure on the country's finished output.
    • GDP=C+I+G+(X−M)

  2. Output method*

    • Never count the output twice
    • GDP= Gross "value added" of various sectors + indirect taxes - subsidies

    Value added*

    • The value of all newly generated goods and services -the value of all goods and services consumed in their creation.

  3. Income Method*

    • all the incomes which have been earned in producing the country’s output

Nominal GDP

  • GDP at current market prices, and so not adjusted to inflation.
  • In most cases higher than actual due to inflation.

Real GDP

  • Nominal GDP adjusted by inflation.

  • Nominal GDP divided by the current year "price index"

    Difficulties of measuring

    1. The activity is on a small scale, hard to register all local businesses, such as local food stands

    2. The activity is illegal, like drug-dealing

    3. The activity is not reported, to evade tax or prevent regulations

      • Lost of tax revenue
      • Official reports might overstate the inflation
      • Subsistence agriculture

Real GDP per capita

  • Real GDP/population size
  • If GDP increase less than population, people’s living standards may fall.
  • might be extremely high because of billionaires.

Policies to promote Econ growth:

Expansionary Fiscal Policy
  • Gov spending rise-jobs created-consumer income rise-consumer spending rise - firms expand production-unemployment falls - real GDP increases- higher Econ growth.
  • Effectiveness depends on the supply side and the size of increase spending and the reduction in income tax rate.
Expansionary Monetary Policy
  • Central bank lower interest rate-cost of borrowing falls-borrowing rises-consumer spending rises-business investment rises- unemployment falls - real GDP increases- higher Econ growth
  • Effectiveness depends on the current level of interest rates
Supply-side policy
  • In the long run, demand side policies might not support sustainable growth.
  • All supply-side policy is viable
Protectionist Policy
  • Trade restrictions, such as tariffs and quotas - cost of imports increase - demand for local substitute rise- higher GDP
  • Effectiveness might be reduced if trading partners also respond.
Exchange rate devaluation
  • Devalue currency - make exports cheaper - higher GDP
  • Imports more expensive-lower import - higher net export

Procedure

Boom

  • Real GDP increases rapidly

Recession

  • A falling output or negative economic growth an economy over a sustained period of time, usually two consecutive quarters.

  • Cause

    • Demand Side

      • Consumption declines

        • Contradictory fiscal/monetary policy
        • Higher unemployment-> lower income
        • Lower consumer confidence

      • Investment declines

        • Contradictory money policy
        • Lower business confidence

      • Gov spending declines

        • Decreased spending on infrastructure and public services

    • Supply Side

      • Decline in AS

        • Natural disasters
        • Outflow of skilled workers

Slump

  • AD in the economy stays low

Why is deflation a problem?

  • Delayed and lower consumption
  • Higher unemployment
  • Higher unemployment spending-> opportunity cost
  • Lower stock of firms
  • Higher real value of debt

Recovery

  • Real GDP began to rise as a result of rising AD

Consequence

  • Long run - increase the quantity of resources
  • Short run - lower consumption of consumer goods

Chapter 30 Employment and Unemployment

  • Employment: the state of having paid work
  • Unemployment: the state of being without paid work, but willing and able to work if a job becomes available.
  • Labour force: The sum of employed people + unemployed people
  • Full employment: a state that almost all people who are willing and able to work are employed, and unemployment rate is kept at the “lowest possible rate”.•

Natural rate of Unemployment

  • Usually 3-4%

  • Reasons

    1. in the process of leaving and finding work
    2. learning new skills due to the changing structure of the economy

Changes in the pattern of employment

1. Industrial Structure

  • Employment moves from primary to secondary to tertiary sector as economies develop

    • Results from

      1. Manufacturing industries relocating abroad to save cost
      2. Rising consumer incomes, leading to higher demand for services
      3. Educated consumer more likely to use banking and insurance
      4. More educated graduated seeking higher paying jobs.
      5. Advances in tech, which reduce the need for labour.

  • Require workers to be occupationally and geographical mobile.

2. Women in labour force

  • Changing social attitudes have resulted more women looking for work and undertaking higher level edu and training.
  • Hence, women are making up higher side proportion of the total labour force.
  • Gender discrimination is dealing in many countries, opening job opportunities in women and raising their wages.

3. Workers employed in the private and private sectors.

  • Less ratio of workers employed by the public sector, while more hired by the private sector.
  • Because countries moving closer to market economies
  • Privatization and deregulation

4. Full time and part time workers

  • Most workers work full-time, while some work part-time

    Reasons for hiring part time workers
    • Flexibility change working time according to demand
    • Saving costs lower wage and not holiday payment
    • Legal considerations might not protected by laws

5. Employed VS Self-employed & Informal VS Formal

  • There are people who get self-employed.

  • Many people work in the informal economy.

    • No access to social security benefits
    • No employment protection

6. High and low quality employment

  • High quality employment : interesting, chances for progress, good working condition and high job security.
  • Proportion of high quality employment tend to increase as economy develops.

7. Flexible employment

  • Adjusts quickly and smoothly to changes in market conditions.
  • Numerical/Temporal/Locational/Functional/Wage Flexibility

8. Youth employment

  • Young people between 15-24 are making up an increasingly high proportion of the unemployed.
  • Lack skills/work experience/qualifications/higher education

 

Changes in the level of unemployment

  • A rise in employment might decrease unemployment, if it is the unemployment who fill at least some of the extra jobs.

  • If labor force increase and grow faster than the number of jobs available, it might increase both employment and unemployment.

  • Sometimes unemployment falls without and increase in employment.

    1. People too old and retire
    2. People go into full-time education
    3. People emigrate

The labour force participation-rate

A rise in the the working age population or The labour force participation rate will increase the labour force.

  • The working age population may increase due to a rise in the birth rate, fall in the death rate or net immigration.
  • The labour force participation rate are the proportion of people who are working age and belong to the labour force.

Economically Inactive people

  • Retired early
  • Prisons, universities or armies
  • Homemakers
  • Long-term sick

The labour market participation-rate

  • Total labour force/total working-age population

  • Influenced by

    • Wage on offer
    • Social attitudes towards working women
    • Provisions for the care of children and the elderly.
    • Social attitudes and provisions for the disabled to work
    • The proportion of school leavers who go into higher edu.

Measure of Unemployment

Claimant count

  • a measure of unemployment which counts as unemployed those in receipt of unemployment benefits.

  • Cheap and quick as the gov knows the amount paid and recipients

  • Disadvantage:

    • Understates unemployment some people who are unemployed might not receive unemployment benefits.

    • e.g.

      • People on gov training schemes
      • People forced to be retired early
      • People who quit or fired.

Labour force survey (LFS)

  • a measure of unemployment which counts as unemployed people who identify as such in a survey.

  • Count people as unemployed if they

    • Without a job
    • Searching for employment in the past month and
    • ready to start working within two weeks

  • Advantage

    • Used to make International comparisons
    • More accurate

  • Problems

    1. Accuracy depend on how questions are asked and interpreted, and whether the sample selected is representative
    2. Take longer to gather data
    3. Information has to be critically examined: counts people as employed if they do any work.

Causes/Types of unemployment

1. Frictional Unemploymentnot-serious

  • temporary unemployment arising from workers being in between jobs, moving from one job to another

  • Reasons for leaving

    • Unhappy
    • No longer needed by employer
    • Want to look for better job

  • unavoidable, non-serious

    Search Unemployment

  • workers who have lost their jobs as looking for a job they are more willing to accept.

Casual Unemployment
  • Workers regularly being between periods of employment
  • e.g. actor /migrant farm workers.

2. Seasonal unemployment

  • the unemployment caused by a fall in demand at particular times of the year
  • Seasonality industries
  • e.g. fruit pickers might be hired during the fruit harvest season.
  • A form of Frictional Unemployment

3. Structural Unemploymentserious

  • Caused by long-term changes in the pattern of demand and methods of production.

  • Decline of certain industries

  • Wide-scale unemployment might exist in primary/secondary sectors as a country develops

  • Reasons

    • Another country become better at producing
    • A substitute is found
    • Capital being substituted for labour
    • Demands for certain products might fall when the country develops

  • Consequences

    • Is a serious concern as it may result in a large number of job losses and might persist longer periods.
    • Might require large amount of retraining schemes.
Regional unemployment
  • unemployment caused by a decline in job opportunities in a particular area of the country
Technological unemployment
  • the unemployment caused by workers being replaced by capital equipment

4. Cyclical unemploymentserious

  • unemployment caused by a lack of Aggregate Demand
  • During recessionary periods - income fall - spending decrease- lower AD- less workers employed
  • The greater the fall in aggregate demand, the more severe is the cyclical unemployment
  • Serious

Stocks of unemployment

  • The number of people unemployed at any one time

  • Influenced by: the rate of flow of people “into” unemployment and the time period for which they are unemployed.

  • Might be higher in

    1. More workers have been dismissed from their jobs
    2. More people have entered the labor force without finding a job
    3. People have been unemployed for a longer period of time

Consequences of unemployment

Individuals

  • A dramatic fall in living standard

  • Long-term unemployment might lead to depression

  • A negative on the education of the children of the unemployed might affect their future.

  • Loss of confidence and the opportunity to get new skills and training

  • Tax payers:

    • Gov might raise tax to pay for unemployment benefits and job creation programs.

Firms

  • Lower income of firms- higher unemployment- lower income- lower spending - fall in sale - hire less - job loss- higher unemployment
  • positive effect: “increased availability of workers at lower wages”

Government

  • Reduced tax revenue

  • Less money to spend on infrastructure

  • Increased spending on unemployment benefits and welfare benefits

    1. High opportunity cost
    2. Higher government debt

The whole economy

  • Reduced efficiency : unemployment is an inefficient use of labour- reduced Econ growth.
  • Increased poverty: main causes of poverty, widen gap between rich and poor.
  • Falling Econ growth
  • Lower inflation/deflation: lower demand-pull inflation or even deflation

Policies fixing unemployment

Frictional

  • Improve the labour market
  • increase the gap between payment and unemployment benefit, by cutting income tax rates and reducing unemployment benefit
  • Increase information on new job vacancies, so the unemployed are more aware of opportunities.

Structual

  • Improve the quality of edu and training/retraining scheme
  • Identify accurately which industries will expand and the skills required.
  • Subsidize new industries

Cyclical

  • Expansionary fiscal and monetary policy
  • Gov need to judge accurately the gap between current level of real GDP and the full employment level.

 

Chapter 31: Inflation and Deflation

Definitions

  • Inflation: a sustained rise in the general price level of good and services as measured by a consumer price index.

    • Low rate not really harmful

  • Hyperinflation: very high rates of inflation that are out of control, causing average prices in the economy to rise rapidly.

  • Deflation: a sustained fall in the general price level of goods and services

  • Disinflation: a fall in the rate of inflation

 

Measurement of Inflation and Deflation

  • CPI:The consumer price index a measure of the “weighted” average of the prices of a representative basket of goods and services.

    • Also used to measure the living cost of average household
    • Weights based on proportion of an average household’s spending on the items
    • used for international comparisons of the inflation rates
    • A benchmark for central bank to decide the interest rate.

 

How to calculate CPI

  1. Selecting a base year

    • select a relatively standard year called base year
    • Base year is given an index of 100.

  2. Finding out how households spend money

    • Because a change in price of a product which people

      • spend a large proportion of their total expenditure
      • most people consume it frequently

      Will have more impact on living cost.

  3. Identify Price changes

    • Each month, government officials find out information about prices

  4. Constructing "weighted price index"

    • “multiply” the “new weight” by the “new price index”

How to work out the weight?

  • Gov officials carry out surveys
  • Percentage on expenditure, not income.
  • Household spending patterns are reviewed each year with new surveys.

 

Causes of inflation

Demand-pull inflation

  • Higher aggregate demand results in demand-pull inflation

    • due to higher consumption, investment, government spending or net exports.

  • If AS can rise to match the rise in AD, might not be d-p inflation

Monetary Inflation
  • excess demand is created by an excessive growth of the money supply

Cost-push inflation

  • Higher cost of production results in cost-push inflation.

  • Reasons

    • Rise in labour costs: ingrain in trade union power or unemployment rate declines OR “wage-price spiral”: inflation -workers ask for higher wages to maintain living standard-even higher inflation!
    • Increase in the cost of raw materials

Negative Consequences of inflation

  1. Loss of currency value: Currency depreciation

    • Hyperinflation causes the value of money may be falling so rapidly, people may lose confidence in using the country's currency.
    • A drop in demand - lower value.

  2. Low and high income earners

    • Inflation harms the poor more than the rich
    • Low income earners have higher PED

  3. Fixed income earners.

    • Fixed income earners are worse off.
    • Fall in real income
    • Necessary to increase wages continuously to fit the inflation

  4. Consumers

    • Lower Purchasing power

  5. Lenders borrowers and savers

    • Lenders may lose money paid back to lenders drop
    • Borrowers may gain paying "less" in real value.
    • Real interest rate = Nominal interest rate - Inflation
    • Savers might also lose if inflation higher, real interest rate could be negative

  6. Menu costs and shoe-leather cost

    • Menu Cost

      • Produces need to be repriced frequently
      • Costy

    • Shoe-leather cost

      • Spend more time searching for best deals
      • Draw money from banks more often

  7. Uncertainty

    • Hard to judge the right price
    • Hard to plan ahead

  8. Balance of payment positions

    • Inflation rate high - less price competitive
    • Fall in export revenue directly
    • Import less certain

  9. Fiscal drag

    • Gov not adjust tax brackets in line with inflation
    • People's incomes are dragged into higher tax brackets

Benefits of inflation

  • Encourage firms to expand - if low and stable demand-pull inflation
  • Reduce the real burden of debt which have already been built up
  • Firms pay lower real value of wages

Policies to control

Demand-pull inflation

  • Gov spending reduced - less jobs - income falls - spending falls - AD falls -lower inflation
  • Income tax rate rise - disposable income falls - spending falls
  • Raise interest rate - cost of borrowing rises - borrowing falls - spending and investment falls
  • Effectiveness depend on confidence
  • Might cause lower economic growth and higher cyclical unemployment

Cost-push inflation

  • Supply side policies
  • Investment in education and training: more educated/skilled labor force—higher labor productivity—lower average cost
  • Increased competition
  • Subsidies to encourage innovations
  • Not effective in short run

 

Causes of deflation

  • Demand side: recession - fall in income and rise in unemployment - lower AD

  • Supply side: "positive supply shock"- a sudden increase in AS

    • When AS rises and the economy is at near full capacity, the general price level might fall- deflation

  • Benign/Good deflation : caused by higher AS

  • Malign/Bad deflation: caused by lower AD

Consequences of deflation

  • Consumers: Expect price to be cheaper in future- delay consumption - drop in AD - increase in unemployment and reduce in economic growth

  • Workers: unemployment high and job security low - people save instead of spend - drop in AD- worsening unemployment and growth

  • Borrowers: lose, as paying back more in real value. Lower borrowing. Higher burden.

  • Lenders: gain

  • Savers : real value of money increases.

  • Firms: provide incentive to increase cash holdings - lower investment and lower AD

  • The economy: Exports cheaper and more price competitive - higher exports- create jobs - lower unemployment and higher AD - better balance of payment

    Malign deflation might cause AD to drop and higher unemployment - downward deflationary spiral

Policies to control deflation

Demand-side

  • Lower interest rate - cost of borrowing falls

  • Increase gov spending - more jobs - higher income

  • Income tax rates reduced - more disposable income

  • Effectiveness depend on consumer confidence and business confidence
